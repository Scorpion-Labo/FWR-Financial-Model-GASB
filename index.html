FutureWorld Robotics — Business Plan

The First Interactive AI Robotics Center · Sorphoan (Jack) Ear, Founder · June 2026 · Confidential — for discussion purposes
1

Executive Summary

FutureWorld Robotics will be the first interactive AI robotics center in the United States — a premium attraction where guests meet lifelike, conversational humanoid robots, including celebrity androids, and journey from the dawn of robotics to the age of AI. Unlike a static wax museum, every figure talks and interacts, and an ever-changing, vendor-funded content engine gives guests a reason to return.

The center opens on a fast-track schedule: a soft opening targeted for Christmas 2026 and a full-scale grand opening in Q1 2027, in a leased, phased 36,000 sq ft facility (24,000 sq ft built first). Seventeen revenue streams — led by admission, on-site spend, and memberships, and complemented by education, exhibitor showcases, sponsorships, and CES-style events — drive projected operating revenue of $35.5M in the first year, rising to $50.8M by year five. As a tax-exempt governmental enterprise fund, the center reports a change in net position rather than net income — income before capital contributions grows from $10.8M to $19.9M, building total net position to $95.0M by year five.

2

The Center — Overview & Vision

FutureWorld Robotics is a premium experiential attraction dedicated entirely to robotics — past, present, and future. Its mission is to make advanced robotics and AI tangible, inspiring, and accessible: to let the public meet, talk to, and learn from lifelike machines, and to inspire the next generation of robotics engineers.

The experience stages a journey from the earliest mechanical automatons and industrial robots through to today's lifelike AI humanoids — a living museum of robotics that doubles as an education center and a stage for the newest inventions. The vision is to establish the category-defining “AI robotics center” brand and expand it over time through new locations, traveling exhibits, and format licensing.

3

The Product & Experience

The heart of FutureWorld Robotics is its fleet of lifelike, conversational robots. A marquee Celebrity Android Hall features humanoid androids of iconic figures that can talk and interact in character; surrounding galleries trace the history of robotics into the AI era, with interactive demonstrations, performer robots, and quadruped robo-animals throughout. The core differentiator is the content engine: the floor is ever-changing and partly vendor-funded, so the experience evolves continuously — driving repeat visits that a fixed attraction cannot match.

Market Opportunity

The United States leads the world in attraction attendance and discretionary entertainment spend, and public fascination with AI and humanoid robots is at an all-time high. FutureWorld Robotics targets a leading destination market; three candidates were analyzed — Los Angeles (~50M annual visitors), Las Vegas (~38.6M, the proven home of premium experiential attractions and CES), and Orlando (~76.7M, the most-visited U.S. destination).

Competitive Analysis & Positioning

Comparable attractions validate demand: every candidate city already hosts a Madame Tussauds, and premium experiential venues such as Sphere and AREA15 prove that audiences pay top prices for a genuine “wow.” FutureWorld Robotics' wedge is clear and ownable — lifelike robots guests can talk to, not static wax — supported by the rotating content engine.

Pricing follows a deliberate skim curve: a premium launch ticket easing over five years as the market broadens, benchmarked against leading U.S. attractions.

Business Model & Revenue Streams

FutureWorld Robotics earns across seventeen streams, which diversifies risk and lifts revenue per visit. The model is consumer-led — admission, on-site spend, and memberships — complemented by higher-margin, faster-growing business lines.

  • Core gate: general & signature admission; memberships & season passes.
  • On-site: photo experiences; retail; robotic café; robotic restaurant; robot vending.
  • Education: courses & field trips; summer & holiday camps; birthday parties.
  • Business & strategic: exhibitor/showcase booths; sponsorship & naming rights; corporate events & private hire; CES-style conference & event hosting; robot-for-hire/mobile shows; grants; traveling-exhibit licensing.

Marketing & Go-to-Market

The go-to-market plan is weighted to where revenue comes from. Consumer demand (gate, on-site, memberships, education) accounts for roughly 89% of Year-1 revenue and is the primary focus; business lines (exhibitors, sponsors, corporate and conference) are a focused, growing secondary track.

  • Tourists: online travel agencies and resellers, hotel and concierge partnerships, and tourism-board co-marketing.
  • Local families & members: digital and social, a founding-membership drive, and community ties.
  • Schools & education: direct district outreach and field-trip programs.
  • Business: direct sales to robotics and technology brands for booths, sponsorships, and events.

The Year-1 marketing budget of $2.5M implies roughly $15 per visit (about 7% of revenue per visit) — an efficient ratio for a premium attraction. The full plan and KPIs are in the appendix.

Operations & Facility

FutureWorld Robotics leases a 36,000 sq ft envelope and builds and opens 24,000 sq ft first, expanding behind a rolling partition over years two to five. Leasing and retrofitting an existing shell — built plain, themed as robots arrive — keeps capital lean and the schedule fast, at a lean ~$333/sq ft. Robots are sourced primarily through established China manufacturing partners for speed and cost, with U.S. manufacturing added over time.

Cost figures are illustrative planning estimates. Actual build-out and construction costs vary with location, market conditions, materials, and finish quality, and should be confirmed with contractor bids before financing.

Risk Analysis & Contingency

The principal risks and mitigations are summarized below; a full scored register is in the appendix.

  • Attendance & willingness-to-pay: 17 streams reduce gate dependence; validated by market sizing and a planned pricing survey.
  • Robot delivery & reliability: established China supply chain, deep roster, dedicated technicians, and a maintenance budget.
  • Likeness rights: license controlled estates and fill the floor with public-domain figures.
  • Geopolitical/China & operating leverage: supplier diversification, a defense firewall, and a flexible cost base.

Legal & Entity Structure

Under the governmental scenario, FutureWorld Robotics is operated as a tax-exempt public enterprise fund — for example an enterprise of, or affiliated with, a public university or public authority — capitalized through a capital contribution and tax-exempt revenue bonds rather than private equity, paired with an affiliated nonprofit education foundation eligible for grants and donations. Robots are sourced from China through arm's-length supplier agreements rather than equity, avoiding foreign-investment (CFIUS) review.

A strict legal and supply-chain firewall separates FutureWorld Robotics from any affiliated defense business, with suppliers screened against U.S. restricted-party and DoD lists, and Section 301 tariffs budgeted. Likeness licensing for celebrity androids is handled through the controlled estates. Final structure will be confirmed with qualified counsel.

Funding Ask & Use of Funds

FutureWorld Robotics is capitalized with $22 million (range $21–23M): a $16M capital contribution and $6M of tax-exempt revenue bonds (interest-only). The capital funds the Center from financing close to a cash-generating operation, with a positive change in net position from the first full year and roughly $98M of cumulative net cash from operating activities over five years.

As a governmental enterprise fund, the center has no private ownership or equity exit; capital is contributed rather than invested for a financial return. Operating surpluses (change in net position) are retained and reinvested in the mission and used to service the revenue bonds, building unrestricted net position over time. Future expansion would be financed through additional capital contributions or tax-exempt debt.
