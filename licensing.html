Licensing Strategy

Public-safe

A robot that looks and performs like a real person, used in a paid attraction, is a commercial use of their identity. The strategy: anchor with a few licensed names, fill the floor with public-domain figures.

The financial model budgets $100,000 per celebrity android — but that is the cost of the robot, not the legal right to portray a real person. Right of publicity protects a person's name, voice, and likeness from unauthorized commercial use; it survives death (70 years in California) and is owned by the estate. The strategy must therefore separate what can be used freely from what must be licensed.

Tier 1 — Public domain & original

Scientists and inventors who died >70 years ago (Einstein likeness, da Vinci, Newton, Tesla, Edison, Lincoln) plus original characters. No cost, no legal risk — the majority of the floor.

🟡

Tier 2 — Licensed marquee estates

A small number of iconic names that drive ticket sales — Monroe (ABG), Bruce Lee (Bruce Lee Enterprises). License only as many as the brand truly needs.

🔴

Tier 3 — Living celebrities

Only with a direct agreement; generally avoid at launch.

The legal framework, in plain terms

Right of publicity protects a person's name, voice, signature, photograph, and likeness from unauthorized commercial use. It survives death — in California (Civil Code 3344.1) it lasts 70 years and is owned by the estate, which is why Monroe and Bruce Lee are still controlled. It is state law and varies, so FutureWorld Robotics' eventual location affects exposure.

Einstein is the exception: a federal court found his publicity right expired ~50 years after his 1955 death, so the likeness is largely public domain — though Hebrew University still licenses the EINSTEIN trademark.
