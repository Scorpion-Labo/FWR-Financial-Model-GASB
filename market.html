Market & Location Analysis

Gated

Three-city comparison — Los Angeles · Las Vegas · Orlando. The market sized on evidence, then weighed on fit, cost, and strategy.

1

Purpose & approach

This analysis sizes the market and assesses location for FutureWorld Robotics across the three leading US destinations for a premium, AI-robot experiential attraction. Rather than pre-selecting a city, it lays the three out side by side so the choice can be made on evidence. Each market is measured on visitor demand, local base, cost, competition, and strategic fit, followed by a TAM / SAM / SOM (Total Addressable Market / Serviceable Available Market / Serviceable Obtainable Market) sizing for each.

Sizing method. TAM is the total annual visitor pool to the metro. SAM applies an assumed 15% attraction-participation rate (the share of leisure visitors who buy a ticket to a paid indoor cultural or experiential attraction) — a conservative planning figure to confirm with local attraction data. SOM is FutureWorld Robotics' modeled attendance (165,000 in Year 1 rising to 270,000 by Year 5), expressed as a capture share of SAM.

Read-through

In every market the plan requires capturing under 1% of total visitors, and only ~2–5% of the attraction-going segment — so market size is not the binding constraint. Las Vegas shows the highest required capture (smaller base) but the audience most primed to buy a premium ticket; Orlando offers the deepest pool but the most competition for attention; Los Angeles offers by far the largest local base for memberships and repeat visits.

4

City notes

🌴

Los Angeles

Strengths: ~50M visitors and a 12.7M local base (the largest membership/repeat-visit pool); closest to the co-founder's robotics company and to robotics/AI engineering talent; the media capital for a launch-PR splash.

Watch-outs: highest rent and the most crowded attraction market; California taxes and operating costs.

🎰

Las Vegas

Strengths: an audience of high-discretionary adults primed for premium paid experiences (Sphere, AREA15 prove the model); home of CES, which feeds the exhibitor-booth and conference-hosting streams; no Nevada state income tax; drivable from California.

Watch-outs: 2025 visitation fell 7.5%; smaller local base for memberships — stress-test attendance against the softness (the A/B/C contingency plan already covers this).

🎢

Orlando

Strengths: the largest visitor pool in the US (76.7M) and a record-setting, growing market; no Florida state income tax; strong family throughput for education and birthday streams.

Watch-outs: Disney and Universal dominate visitor attention and wallet; thinner robotics talent; farthest from the California base.

5

Assumption check vs. the financial model

Rent ($45/sq ft/yr): defensible as a tourist-corridor figure in all three. It sits mid-range for a strong Las Vegas location, fits Orlando's prime I-Drive corridor, and is tight-but-workable for Los Angeles (where prime space runs higher, so an off-prime entertainment-district site would be needed to hold the number).

Attendance (165k to 270k): supported by the sizing above in every market — a low single-digit share of attraction-goers. The most demanding case is Las Vegas (highest required capture of a softening base); the most cushioned is Orlando (largest pool). A local willingness-to-pay survey in the chosen city remains the key validation step.

6

How to weigh the choice

  • Pick Las Vegas if the priority is audience fit, premium pricing power, CES/exhibitor synergy, and a low-tax, drivable location.
  • Pick Los Angeles if proximity to robotics & AI talent, the largest membership base, and media/PR reach matter most.
  • Pick Orlando if maximum visitor volume and family throughput outweigh the intense theme-park competition and distance from base.
